# Philosophy Questions: What Is the Definition of Knowledge?

# [Knowledge](../Definitions/Knowledge.md)

- Historic definition: "justified true belief." 

- Whatever is true, believed, and reasonable.

- We know through our ability to reason/rationalize, and their physical senses.

- "Justified" means backed up with enough reasonable evidence.

## How do we know when a belief is true or false?

- In a conversation with mathematician [Theaetus](../People/Theaetus.md), [Socrates](../People/Socrates.md) reasoned that knowledge wasn't simply "true belief" because sometimes people have accidental true beliefs.

- [Edmund Gettier](../People/Edmund%20Gettier.md) argued that there were also rare cases of justified true belief that wasn't necessarily knowledge.

    > Here’s one of Gettier’s examples: Smith and Jones are applying for a job. The CEO tells Smith that Jones will get the job. Smith also discovers that Jones has ten coins in his pocket. Smith puts together the evidence and arrives at the belief that a person with 10 coins in his pocket will get the job. Here’s the twist: Smith will actually end up getting the job, and Smith also happens to have ten coins in his pocket. So, Smith’s belief that the job will go to a person with 10 coins is true *and* justified (Smith has good reasons for believing this). And yet, does this really count as *knowledge*?

- [Skepticism](../Definitions/Skepticism.md) came onto the scene during [the Renaissance](../Events/the%20Renaissance.md), bringing an agnostic approach to knowledge that says with certainty that knowledge is unobtainable.

    - Con 1: how can you know you don't know anything without knowing anything?

    - Con 2: knowing nothing isn't more comforting than knowing something, it certainly doesn't bring any peace of mind.

## With so many competing options, can any of our beliefs be justified?

- [Rene Descartes](../People/Rene%20Descartes.md)

    - Refuted skepticism by saying there were things we could know for certain, like arithmetic and geometry, and we should focus on those things.

    - Came up with the "I think...therefore I am!" ideology.

    - “I assuredly existed, since I was persuaded…I exist, since I am deceived…this proposition I am, I exist, is necessarily true each time it is expressed by me, or conceived in my mind.”

    - The idea of God and the infinite must have been planted by God himself, therefore, God exists. After all, the perfection of existing is impossible if a perfect God does not exist.

    > Thus, through his ontological argument for the existence of God, Descartes went from doubting everything, to believing in his own self, to believing in God. Once past that threshold, things began speeding up. After all, if God exists and is perfect, and if it is an imperfection to deceive, then God would not deceive us about the world around us. We could *know* all these things, with a knowledge perched safely on certainty.
