# Ch 2. Scientific Reasoning

[Ch. 1 What is Science?](./Ch%201%20What%20is%20Science.md)

- [Deductive inference](../Definitions/deduction.md) -> if the premises have an appropriate relation, the conclusion is true. The premises may not be factually true, but that doesn't matter.

    - If this, than that.

    - Guarantees that if we start with factually true premises, we will end up with a factually true conclusion

- [Inductive inference](../Definitions/induction.md) -> moving from premises about examined objects to a conclusion about unexamined objects. The conclusion isn't certain based on premises.

    - If this, than MAYBE that.

    - Can't guarantee a factually true conclusion, true premises could lead to false conclusions.

    - We do it everyday, like when we reason without facts (e.g. "I always do it this way, so it will work this time."

    - Scientists also use inductive reasoning, especially in group experiments.

- [Karl Popper](../People/Karl%20Popper.md) argued that scientists don't need [inductive inferences](../Definitions/induction.md), only [deductive ones](../Definitions/deduction.md). Basically, he argued that you can't prove a theory to be true from a limited data sample, but you can prove it's false. A theory only needs one contradiction to be false, and you can find that in a limited data sample; therefore, a scientist can use deductive reasoning to disprove theories. Unfortunately, Popper didn't take into account that most scientists are not purely interested in disproving theories, but in proving their own.

- [David Hume](../People/David%20Hume.md) (1711-1776) argued that [inductive reasoning](../Definitions/induction.md) could not be rationally justified at all. Induction is so popular only because we have made it a habit, and we have no reasonable reason why.

- Uniformity of nature -> the assumption that objects we have examined will be similar to the same sort of objects that we haven't examined.

    - Has no empirical evidence to support it. The only way we could argue for the support of induction is to say that its worked this long so there must be something good about it. However, that would be using induction to prove induction! Therefore, the uniformity of nature can't be proven, it's a circular argument.

    - One response to this problem is probability---it is probable that the premises of an inductive inference will lead to this conclusion. Still, this is an issue within itself.

    - Another response is that induction is so fundamental to our thinking and reasoning that it can't be justified.

- Peter Trawson defended induction by saying that just how someone would only examine the lawbooks to see if an action was legal, and not law itself, the same is of induction.

- Inference to the best explanation -> taking two or more premises and forming a conclusion using the best possible explanation.

    - Examples: Darwin's theory of evolution, Einstien's kinetic theory.

    - Accoridng to IBE, how do we determine which hypothesis is better? The simplest or the most parsimonious one; the hypothesis that accounts for the most data. But, how do we know the universe is not complex?

- There are different kinds of probability:

    - Frequency interpretation -> equates probabilities with proprtions or frequencies.

    - Subjective interpretation -> probability is the strength of one's personal opinions.

    - Logical interpretation -> probability is the measure of strength of the supporting evidence.

- Probability is important in philosophy of science because (1) many branches of science love laws and theories that use probabilityes, and (2) it might shed some light on induction.

- Frequency interpretation is itself dependant on induction, subject interpretation denies objectivity, and the logical interpretation has many problems within itself.
