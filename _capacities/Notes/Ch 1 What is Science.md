# Ch. 1 What is Science?

- [philosophy of science](../Definitions/philosophy%20of%20science.md) seeks to understand how experimentation, observation, theory-construction, and other techniques have helped scientists understand the world we live in.

- [The scientific revolution](../Zettels/The%20scientific%20revolution.md) -> 1500-1750

- [The Copernican revolution](../Zettels/The%20Copernican%20revolution.md) -> began in 1542 when [Nicolas Copernicus](../People/Nicolas%20Copernicus.md) (1473-1543) attacked the [Geocentric model](../Zettels/Geocentric%20model.md) of the universe and replaced it with the [Heliocentric model](../Zettels/Heliocentric%20model.md). It eventually led to the development of [modern physics](../Definitions/modern%20physics.md).

- [Johannes Kepler](../People/Johannes%20Kepler.md) (1571-1630) and [Galileo Galilei](../People/Galileo%20Galilei.md) (1564-1642) developed [modern physics](../Definitions/modern%20physics.md).

- [Galileo Galilei](../People/Galileo%20Galilei.md) developed the [law of free-fall](../Zettels/law%20of%20free-fall.md) and a [theory of mechanics](../Zettels/theory%20of%20mechanics.md). He was also the first modern physicist, first to use mathematics for objects in the material world, and founder of the [empirical approach](../Definitions/empirical%20approach.md).

- [Rene Descartes](../People/Rene%20Descartes.md) (1596-1650) developed a mechanical philosophy, stating that the physical world is made up of particles of matter that interact and collide with each other.

- [Isaac Newton](../People/Isaac%20Newton.md) (1643-1727), wrote *Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy,* and improved on [Descartes' laws of motion](../AtomicNotes/Descartes'%20laws%20of%20motion.md) and [Descartes' rules of collision](../Zettels/Descartes'%20rules%20of%20collision.md) with his own [three laws of motion](../AtomicNotes/Newton's%20Laws%20of%20Motion.md) and the [Principle of universal gravitation](../Zettels/Principle%20of%20universal%20gravitation.md).

- [relativity theory](../Definitions/relativity%20theory.md), developed by [Albert Einstein](../People/Albert%20Einstein.md), shows how [Newtonian mechanics](../AtomicNotes/Newton's%20Laws%20of%20Motion.md) doesn't work for very massive or high velocity objects.

- [quantum mechanics](../Definitions/quantum%20mechanics.md) shows how [Newtonian mechanics](../AtomicNotes/Newton's%20Laws%20of%20Motion.md) doesn't work for subatomic particles either.

- [Charles Darwin](../People/Charles%20Darwin.md) wrote *The Origin of Species* (1859), introducing the [theory of evolution](../Definitions/evolution.md) by [natural selection](../Definitions/natural%20selection.md).

- [Francis Crick](../People/Francis%20Crick.md) and [James D. Watson](../People/James%20D%20Watson.md) discovered the double helix structure of [DNA](../Definitions/DNA.md), launching the field of [molecular biology](../Definitions/molecular%20biology.md).

- [Rene Descartes](../People/Rene%20Descartes.md), [Isaac Newton](../People/Isaac%20Newton.md), and [Albert Einstein](../People/Albert%20Einstein.md) all contributed to [philosophy of science](../Definitions/philosophy%20of%20science.md).

- [philosophy of science](../Definitions/philosophy%20of%20science.md) studies the methods of science by asking questions such as how should science proceed? what methods should be used? how confident should we be about the results? and are their limits to science?

- [Karl Popper](../People/Karl%20Popper.md) believed that a [scientific theory](../Definitions/scientific%20theory.md) should be falsifiable (testable; able to be proven false), and those that weren't are [pseudo-science](../Definitions/pseudo-science.md) (like [Freud's psychoanalytic theory](../Zettels/Freud's%20psychoanalytic%20theory.md) and [Marx's theory of history](../Zettels/Marx's%20theory%20of%20history.md)).

- [Popper](../People/Karl%20Popper.md) argued that any theory that seemed to make up excuses so it could explain away contradiction discoveries and stay relevant is not real science, but really [pseudo-science](../Definitions/pseudo-science.md).

    - **Issue:** what kinds of ignored conflicting data should make a [scientific theory](../Definitions/scientific%20theory.md) [pseudo-science](../Definitions/pseudo-science.md)? Scientists don't write off a theory the moment there's a contradiction, only when the contradictions pile up. So, when does the theory become pseudoscience? And, is there some common feature shared between "true science" by which we can test for science or pseudoscience?

    - [Ludwig Wittgenstein](../People/Ludwig%20Wittgenstein.md) says no, because just like games are only loosely bound together by a cluster of features, so is science. Finding a fixed set of features to define science is impossible.
